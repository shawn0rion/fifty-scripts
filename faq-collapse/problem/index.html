So you want to ace the interview...

What Programming Languages Are You Fluent In?
As a web developer, it's paramount to have a strong grasp of certain programming languages. The most common ones include HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and perhaps a backend language like Python or Ruby. The depth of your understanding and your ability to apply these languages effectively to create intuitive, interactive websites will be a key focus during your first interview.
How Familiar Are You With Responsive Design?
With an increasing number of users accessing websites from mobile devices, it's essential for any web developer to be well-versed in responsive design. This ensures that your website will look and function well, irrespective of the device it's viewed on. During your interview, you may be asked to provide examples of your experience with responsive design or even to demonstrate your understanding in real-time.
Can You Describe Your Experience With Version Control Systems?
Version control systems like Git are integral to modern web development. They enable developers to work together on projects without overwriting each other's changes. In your first interview, expect to discuss your experience with version control systems, including any collaborative projects you've been a part of.
How Do You Approach Testing and Debugging?
A key part of web development is ensuring that the end product is free from errors and performs as expected. Consequently, your first interview is likely to include questions about your testing and debugging strategies. You might be asked to describe your process, discuss specific tools you've used, or even to demonstrate your debugging skills during the interview.
What's Your Experience With APIs?
APIs are a key component of many modern web applications, allowing different software to communicate and share data. In your interview, you'll likely be asked about your experiences with APIs. This could involve discussing APIs you've worked with in the past, demonstrating how you'd use an API in a given scenario, or explaining how you'd approach troubleshooting issues with an API.
